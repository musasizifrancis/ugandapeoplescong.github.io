UPC HISTORICAL TIMELINE

A journey through the significant events that shaped the Uganda People's Congress from its founding to the present day

1952

Formation of Uganda National Congress

The roots of Uganda People's Congress (UPC) can be traced to the Uganda National Congress (UNC), which was formed under the leadership of Ignatius Musazi.

The UNC emerged out of Uganda African Farmers Union (UAFU), which was a movement of small holder farmers agitated for better prices of coffee and cotton.

1960

UPC Officially Founded

The Uganda People's Congress was officially formed as a political party by Milton Obote and other political leaders from various parts of Uganda.

The party was established to unite non-Baganda populations under a single political entity to counter Buganda's dominance.

1962

Independence and First Government

UPC formed an alliance with Kabaka Yekka (KY) and won the pre-independence elections. Milton Obote became the first Prime Minister of independent Uganda.

Uganda gained independence from British colonial rule on October 9, with UPC at the helm of government.

1966

Constitutional Crisis

The UPC-KY alliance collapsed. Milton Obote suspended the constitution and assumed all government powers.

The Kabaka's palace was stormed by government forces led by Idi Amin, forcing the Kabaka into exile.

1967

Republican Constitution

A new constitution was introduced that abolished traditional kingdoms and established Uganda as a republic with an executive presidency.

Milton Obote became the first Executive President of Uganda under the new constitution.

1971

Military Coup

Idi Amin led a military coup that overthrew the UPC government while President Obote was attending a Commonwealth meeting in Singapore.

The UPC was banned along with all other political parties, and many party leaders were forced into exile.

1979-1980

Fall of Amin and UPC Revival

After the fall of Idi Amin's regime, the UPC reorganized under Milton Obote's leadership in preparation for elections.

The party contested in the 1980 general elections against the Democratic Party (DP) and other political groups.

1980

Second UPC Government

UPC won the general elections, and Milton Obote became President for a second time (Obote II), forming the second UPC government.

This election was controversial, with allegations of rigging, which led to Yoweri Museveni's decision to launch a guerrilla war.

1985

Military Coup and Overthrow

The second UPC government was overthrown in a military coup led by General Tito Okello. Milton Obote fled to exile in Zambia.

The coup marked the beginning of another period of political instability in Uganda.

1986-2005

Opposition Years

Following the takeover by Yoweri Museveni's National Resistance Movement (NRM), UPC operated as an opposition party with limited political space.

After Milton Obote's death in 2005, the party entered a period of leadership transition and internal reorganization.

2010

Leadership Changes

The party experienced internal divisions and leadership challenges as it sought to redefine its role in Uganda's politics.

Various factions emerged within the party, each claiming legitimacy and control of the party structure.

2015

Jimmy Akena's Leadership

Jimmy Akena, son of Milton Obote, was elected as the UPC president, bringing new energy and direction to the party.

His leadership marked a new chapter in the party's history, with efforts to rebuild the party structures and increase its national relevance.

2021

General Elections Participation

UPC participated in the general elections, fielding candidates at various levels despite challenges in the political environment.

The party continued to advocate for democratic values and socio-economic development in Uganda.
