THE FOUNDING OF UPC

Exploring the roots and origins of Uganda People's Congress

Formation and Early Beginnings

Early UPC members meeting in the early 1960s

The Uganda People's Congress (UPC) was officially formed in 1960 when the Uganda National Congress (UNC) merged with the Uganda People's Union (UPU). The party was founded by Dr. Apollo Milton Obote who became its first president and later the first Prime Minister of independent Uganda.

The roots of UPC can be traced back to 1952 when the Uganda National Congress (UNC) was formed under the leadership of Ignatius Musazi. The UNC emerged from the Uganda African Farmers Union (UAFU), a movement of smallholder farmers who were agitating for better prices for their coffee and cotton crops.

At this time, most economic and political activities were concentrated in Buganda, and this was reflected in the early leadership of these political organizations. However, as the movement for independence gained momentum across Uganda, the composition and character of the party's leadership began to change.

Path to Independence

As Uganda moved toward independence from British colonial rule, the UPC positioned itself as a nationalist party committed to a united and independent Uganda. The party's ideology was rooted in socialist principles with a strong emphasis on nationalism and anti-colonialism.

In the pre-independence elections of 1962, the UPC formed an alliance with Kabaka Yekka (KY), a royalist party from Buganda. This strategic alliance helped the UPC secure victory and form the first government of independent Uganda, with Milton Obote as Prime Minister.

Independence celebrations, October 9, 1962

On October 9, 1962, Uganda gained independence from Britain with the UPC at the helm of the new nation. The party's early policies focused on Africanization of the civil service, economic development, and balancing the interests of the different regions and ethnic groups in Uganda.

Founding Principles and Ideology

From its inception, the UPC embraced several core principles:

  • Nationalism: The party championed a united Uganda, transcending tribal and regional divisions.
  • Democracy: UPC advocated for democratic governance and universal suffrage.
  • Socialism: Economic policies were initially oriented toward socialist principles, emphasizing collective ownership and equitable distribution of resources.
  • Anti-colonialism: The party strongly opposed colonial rule and sought to dismantle colonial economic structures.
  • Pan-Africanism: UPC supported continental unity and solidarity among African nations.

The party symbol – the hand with a finger pointing forward – represented progress and the path toward a brighter future for Uganda. The party colors of red and blue symbolized courage and loyalty respectively.

Founding Figures

Dr. Apollo Milton Obote - First President of UPC and first Prime Minister of independent Uganda

Ignatius Musazi - Founder of Uganda National Congress, a precursor to UPC

John Kakonge - Early UPC Secretary-General

Grace Ibingira - Key founding member

Key Dates

1952: Formation of Uganda National Congress

1960: Official formation of UPC

1962: UPC forms government after independence

1966: Constitutional crisis and abolition of traditional kingdoms

1969: UPC becomes the sole legal party
