THE HISTORY OF UGANDA PEOPLE'S CONGRESS

The Uganda People's Congress (UPC) is one of Uganda's oldest political parties, with a rich history dating back to the pre-independence era. Formed in 1960, the party has played a crucial role in Uganda's political landscape for over six decades.

1952

The roots of Uganda People's Congress (UPC) can be traced to the Uganda National Congress (UNC), which was formed under the leadership of Ignatius Musazi. The UNC emerged out of Uganda African Farmers Union (UAFU), which was a movement of small holder farmers agitated for better prices of coffee and cotton.

1959

Internal conflicts within the UNC led to its split, with Milton Obote leading one faction. This faction would later form the foundation of the UPC.

March 1960

The Uganda People's Congress was officially formed as a national political party under the leadership of Dr. Apollo Milton Obote. The party was formed to oppose Buganda's separatism and promote nationalism.

1961-1962

UPC formed a coalition with Kabaka Yekka (KY) to counter the Democratic Party (DP) in the pre-independence elections. This strategic alliance helped UPC secure victory.

October 9, 1962

Uganda gained independence with Milton Obote as Prime Minister in a UPC-KY coalition government. Kabaka Mutesa II became the ceremonial President.

1966

The UPC-KY alliance collapsed, and Obote orchestrated a new constitution that abolished traditional kingdoms. Obote became executive President, and UPC became the sole ruling party.

First UPC Government (1962-1971)

During its first tenure in government, UPC under Obote implemented various policies aimed at national integration and economic development. The party promoted a socialist ideology, advocating for the nationalization of key industries and the expansion of public services. This period saw significant investments in education, healthcare, and infrastructure development across Uganda.

Military Rule and UPC in Opposition (1971-1979)

In January 1971, Idi Amin led a military coup that overthrew the UPC government. Many UPC leaders, including Milton Obote, fled into exile, primarily to Tanzania. During this period, UPC operated from exile, organizing resistance against Amin's brutal dictatorship.

Second UPC Government (1980-1985)

Following the overthrow of Idi Amin in 1979, Uganda held general elections in 1980, which UPC won amid allegations of electoral fraud. This marked the beginning of Obote's second term as President. This period was characterized by civil conflict, particularly with Yoweri Museveni's National Resistance Army (NRA) waging a guerrilla war against the government.

Modern Era UPC

After the overthrow of the second UPC government in 1985, the party remained a significant opposition force in Uganda's political landscape. Over the years, UPC has gone through various leadership changes and has participated in multiple elections, maintaining its position as one of Uganda's historic political parties.

Today, under the leadership of Jimmy Akena, the son of Milton Obote, UPC continues to advocate for democratic governance, social justice, and national unity, staying true to its founding principles while adapting to Uganda's evolving political environment.
